What is Pythagoras' theorem?

In every right-angled triangle, the square of the longest side is equal to the sum of the squares of the other two sides. That single sentence — true for every right triangle that has ever existed — is the engine of this whole unit.

The theorem is named after Pythagoras of Samos, a Greek mathematician who lived around 500 BCE. He didn't discover the relationship — Babylonian, Indian, and Chinese mathematicians had been using it for centuries before him. What Pythagoras (or the school named after him) is credited with is the first known proof: a logical argument showing the relationship must hold for any right triangle, not just the ones you happen to test.

The relationship lets you do something powerful. If you know two sides of a right triangle, you can find the third without measuring it. That makes the theorem the foundation of distance, navigation, construction, screen sizes, and almost every problem where two perpendicular distances meet.

TL;DR

Right triangle. Two short sides squared, added. Equals the long side squared. That is the whole theorem. Everything else in this unit is either applying it, proving it, or working with the surds (square roots) it produces.

Anatomy of a right-angled triangle

Before you can apply the theorem, you must be able to look at any right triangle and instantly identify its three named parts. Mixing them up is the most common reason students get the wrong answer.

The three named parts: two legs (a, b) meeting at the right angle, and the hypotenuse (c) opposite it.

The right angle

The 90° corner. You mark it with a small square inside the corner — that little square is how you signal "this is a right angle" on every diagram you draw. Without that mark, the examiner cannot assume the triangle is right-angled, and Pythagoras' theorem doesn't apply to anything else.

The legs (a and b)

The two shorter sides that meet at the right angle. They are also called the opposite and adjacent sides when you start trigonometry next year — but for Pythagoras, you can simply call them legs. Their roles are interchangeable in the formula: it does not matter which one you call a and which one you call b.

The hypotenuse (c)

The side opposite the right angle. It is always the longest side in the triangle. It is always the slanted side — never one of the two sides that touches the right angle. If your "hypotenuse" is shorter than one of the legs, you have identified the wrong side, and your answer will be wrong.

Sanity check: The hypotenuse is always longer than either leg. After every calculation, check that your c is bigger than your a and b. If it isn't, you've mixed up which side is which.

The formula

a² + b² = c² where c is the hypotenuse and a, b are the two legs

Read it out loud: "a squared plus b squared equals c squared." That sentence should become automatic. On every Pythagoras question — every single one — you will write this formula on its own line before substituting any numbers. It's not optional. It earns marks under both Criterion A (correct method) and Criterion C (clear communication), and it forces you to stop and think about which side is which before you start calculating.

Two question types you'll meet

Every Pythagoras question is a variation of one of these two:

TypeGivenWhat you doFind
Type 1Both legs (a and b)Add the squared legs, then square rootThe hypotenuse (c)
Type 2The hypotenuse + one legSubtract the leg² from c², then square rootThe other leg
The single biggest error: using ADD when you should SUBTRACT. If the question gives you the hypotenuse, you are in Type 2 — you must subtract. Do not blindly square and add every number you see.

Why it works — visual proof

Memorising a formula is not the same as understanding why it must be true. Here is the classical visual proof: build a square on each side of the triangle, and the sum of the two smaller squares equals the area of the largest. Click the button below to watch the small squares rearrange themselves to fill the big one.

For a 3–4–5 triangle: 9 + 16 = 25.

The numerical result you just saw — that 3² + 4² (which is 9 + 16) really does equal 5² (which is 25) — is not a coincidence. It works for every right triangle, in every culture, at any time. The relationship is a logical truth, like 2 + 2 = 4: it would be true even if no human had ever written it down.

DEEPER PROOF

Build a square of side (a + b). You can tile it two ways: as one big (a + b)² square, or as four right triangles plus a smaller square of side c. Setting the two areas equal gives a² + 2ab + b² = 2ab + c², which simplifies to a² + b² = c².

Step-by-step worked examples

Example 1 — Find the hypotenuse

A right triangle has legs of 6 cm and 8 cm. Find the hypotenuse.

Step 1 — write the theorem:
a² + b² = c²

Step 2 — substitute:
6² + 8² = c²
36 + 64 = c²

Step 3 — solve:
c² = 100
c = √100 = 10 cm

Step 4 — final answer:
∴ The hypotenuse is 10 cm long.

Example 2 — Find a missing leg

A right triangle has hypotenuse 13 cm and one leg 5 cm. Find the other leg.

Step 1 — write the theorem:
a² + b² = c²

Step 2 — substitute (hypotenuse goes on its own as c):
a² + 5² = 13²
a² + 25 = 169

Step 3 — solve (SUBTRACT):
a² = 169 − 25 = 144
a = √144 = 12 cm

Step 4 — final answer:
∴ The other leg is 12 cm long.

Example 3 — Surd answer (most real questions)

A right triangle has legs 4 cm and 6 cm. Find the hypotenuse, leaving your answer as a simplified surd.

a² + b² = c²
4² + 6² = c²
16 + 36 = c²
c² = 52
c = √52 = √(4 × 13) = 2√13 cm

Final answer:
∴ The hypotenuse is 2√13 cm.

Example 4 — Real-world (ladder against a wall)

A 10 m ladder leans against a wall. Its foot is 6 m from the wall. How far up the wall does it reach?

The ladder is the hypotenuse (c = 10 m), the distance along the ground is one leg (b = 6 m), and the height up the wall is the unknown leg (a). This is a Type 2 problem.

a² + b² = c²
a² + 6² = 10²
a² + 36 = 100
a² = 64
a = 8 m

∴ The ladder reaches 8 m up the wall.

Common Pythagorean triples

A Pythagorean triple is a set of three whole numbers (a, b, c) that satisfy a² + b² = c². Memorising the most common ones saves time on tests — when you see two of these numbers in a question, the third is the answer with no calculation needed.

3 — 4 — 5
9 + 16 = 25
5 — 12 — 13
25 + 144 = 169
8 — 15 — 17
64 + 225 = 289
7 — 24 — 25
49 + 576 = 625
9 — 40 — 41
81 + 1600 = 1681
20 — 21 — 29
400 + 441 = 841

Multiples of triples are also triples

If you double, triple, or scale every number in a triple by the same factor, the result is still a triple. So 3-4-5 generates 6-8-10, 9-12-15, 30-40-50, and so on. This is a Criterion B-style observation: the rule preserves the Pythagorean relationship because squaring distributes over multiplication: (ka)² + (kb)² = k²(a² + b²) = k²c² = (kc)².

Spotting trick: If you see the numbers 6 and 8 in a right-triangle question, the hypotenuse is probably 10 (it's just 3-4-5 doubled). If you see 9 and 12, the hypotenuse is 15 (3-4-5 tripled). Train your eye for these patterns.

The bridge concept — Pythagoras with surds

Most real Pythagoras questions don't give nice whole-number answers. You square the legs, add them, and end up with something like c² = 13 or c² = 52. Taking the square root gives a surd — an exact value that cannot be simplified to a whole number. This is where the two halves of the unit (Pythagoras and radicals) come together.

What is a surd?

A surd is a square root that does not simplify to a whole number — its decimal goes on forever without repeating. Examples: √2, √3, √5, √7, √13. We leave answers as surds because surds are exact, while decimals are approximations. On the Criterion A test, an answer of √13 is correct; an answer of 3.605551275… is rounded.

The five surd rules

RuleIn symbolsExample
Multiply√a × √b = √(ab)√3 × √5 = √15
Divide√a ÷ √b = √(a/b)√20 ÷ √5 = √4 = 2
Simplify√(k²·m) = k√m√50 = √(25·2) = 5√2
Add / subtract (LIKE only)k√n + j√n = (k+j)√n3√2 + 5√2 = 8√2
Square a surd(√a)² = a, (k√a)² = k²a(3√5)² = 9 × 5 = 45

Method: simplifying a surd

The goal is always to pull out the largest perfect square factor. Use the perfect squares — 4, 9, 16, 25, 36, 49, 64, 81, 100, 121, 144 — as your factor list.

Simplify √72:
Largest perfect square factor of 72: 36 (since 72 = 36 × 2)
√72 = √(36 × 2) = √36 × √2 = 6√2
Common slip: √72 = √(9 × 8) = 3√8 is correct but NOT fully simplified — √8 still has a perfect square factor (4). Always check the leftover number under the root has no remaining perfect square factor.

Adding surds — simplify first, then combine

Calculate √8 + √18:
√8 = 2√2, √18 = 3√2
Now LIKE surds: 2√2 + 3√2 = 5√2

Pythagoras in 3D

3D problems usually ask for the long diagonal of a box (cuboid) — the line from one bottom corner, straight through the inside, to the opposite top corner. You can't apply Pythagoras directly because no single right triangle contains all three dimensions. So you apply Pythagoras twice.

The two-step method (use this on tests)

Step 1: find the diagonal of the BASE (a flat rectangle with length a and width b). Call it d. This applies Pythagoras once: d² = a² + b².

Step 2: now use d (the floor diagonal) and h (the height) as the two legs of a NEW right triangle. Its hypotenuse is the long 3D diagonal D. Apply Pythagoras a second time: D² = d² + h².

Find the long diagonal of a 4 × 3 × 12 box:

Step 1 — base diagonal (4 × 3 rectangle):
d² = 4² + 3² = 16 + 9 = 25
d = 5 cm

Step 2 — long diagonal:
D² = 5² + 12² = 25 + 144 = 169
D = 13 cm

The shortcut formula (use this to check)

D² = a² + b² + c² where a, b, c are length, width, height; D is the long diagonal

It comes from substituting Step 1 into Step 2: D² = d² + h² = (a² + b²) + c². Check the worked example above with the shortcut: 4² + 3² + 12² = 16 + 9 + 144 = 169 ✓.

Tip: Show the two-step method on tests — each step uses the standard a² + b² = c² formula, which earns Criterion C (communication) marks. Use the shortcut as a check at the end.

The three traps — most common mistakes

Trap 1: Treating √(a + b) as √a + √b

This is the single biggest surd error. The square root does NOT distribute over addition. Test it with numbers: √(9 + 16) = √25 = 5. But √9 + √16 = 3 + 4 = 7. Different. Always evaluate what's inside the root first.

Trap 2: Adding unlike surds

You can only combine surds that have the same number under the root. So 3√2 + 5√2 = 8√2 ✓, but 3√2 + 5√3 stays as 3√2 + 5√3 — there is no way to combine them.

Trap 3: Stopping before fully simplified

√48 = 2√12 is technically correct, but √12 still has a perfect square factor (4). Keep going: √48 = √(16 × 3) = 4√3. Always sanity-check that the leftover surd has no perfect square factor.

Bonus trap: using a calculator decimal too early. If the question doesn't say "round to 2 d.p.", leave your answer as a surd. Premature decimals lose Criterion A marks for accuracy.

The 6-step working template

Use this structure on every Pythagoras question on the Criterion A + C test. The right-hand tags show which criterion each step is earning marks for. Skipping any step is how students lose easy Criterion C marks while still getting the right number.

  1. Draw and label the diagram. Mark the right angle. Label the hypotenuse. [Criterion C — representation]
  2. Define your variables. "Let c be the hypotenuse," "Let x be the unknown side." [Criterion C — notation]
  3. State the theorem in symbols: a² + b² = c². Write it BEFORE substituting numbers. [Criterion A — method, Criterion C — communication]
  4. Substitute the numbers. Don't skip this line, even if it looks obvious. [Criterion A — method]
  5. Simplify and solve. Each step on its own line. If you take a square root, write the surd before deciding whether to simplify or use a decimal. [Criterion A — accuracy]
  6. Final answer in a sentence with units. e.g., "The ladder is 4√2 m long." Round only if the question tells you to. [Criterion C — communication in context]

📌 The cheat sheet

CRITERION B — FINDING PATTERNS

When the question says: investigate, find a rule, show that, prove that

Do these 5 things:

  1. Try at least 5 different examples.
  2. Look at your numbers. What stays the same?
  3. Write the rule in words.
  4. Write the rule using letters (like d = s√2).
  5. Test a NEW example. Then write WHY the rule works.

The big move: Explain WHY the rule must be true.

CRITERION D — REAL-WORLD PROBLEMS

When the question says: apply, design, calculate, how far, how high

Write 4 things in your answer:

  1. What you're GIVEN. What's ASKED. What DOESN'T MATTER.
  2. Pick Pythagoras 2D or 3D. Say WHY it fits.
  3. Show your working line by line. Add units.
  4. Check the answer makes sense AND list 2 things your model leaves out.

The big move: List 2 things your model leaves out.

TWO SENTENCES TO USE EVERY TIME

Criterion B answer ends with: "My rule must be true because..."

Criterion D answer ends with: "My model assumes..."

Criterion B — Finding patterns

Criterion B is about discovering patterns in numbers or shapes. The marker wants to see HOW you found the pattern, not just the answer.

How to actually see the pattern

If you have 5 examples and still can't see the pattern, try these:

  • Subtract one value from the next. Are the gaps the same?
  • Divide one value by the next. Are the ratios the same?
  • Look at the SQUARES of your numbers (sometimes the pattern hides in the squares).
  • Try dividing the answer by the input. If you get the same number every time, that's your rule.
  • Try a wild example (input = 1, or input = 100). Extremes often show the pattern.
THE 'DIVIDE BY INPUT' TRICK

The most useful pattern-finding trick. Example: diagonal of a square.

s = 1, d = √2, d/s = √2
s = 2, d = 2√2, d/s = √2
s = 3, d = 3√2, d/s = √2

The ratio is always √2 — so the rule is d = √2 × s. Found it instantly.

Sample 1 — Pattern in a shape

Question: Investigate how the diagonal d of a square depends on its side length s. Use s = 1, 2, 3, 4, 5. Find a rule. Justify why it must be true.

Sample answer:

Set up: A diagonal cuts the square in half and makes two right triangles. The diagonal is the hypotenuse with both legs = s.

5 examples: s=1 → d=√2; s=2 → d=2√2; s=3 → d=3√2; s=4 → d=4√2; s=5 → d=5√2.

Pattern (words): Every diagonal is the side × √2.

Rule: d = s√2.

Check: s=6 → predict 6√2. Calculate: d² = 36 + 36 = 72, d = 6√2 ✓.

WHY it must be true: For any square, both legs equal s. By Pythagoras: d² = s² + s² = 2s². Taking the square root: d = s√2. This isn't a coincidence — it follows from Pythagoras every time.

Limit: The rule only works for squares. Rectangles with different sides need a different rule.

Sample 2 — Pattern in numbers

Question: If you double every number in a Pythagorean triple, is the result still a triple? Investigate and justify.

Sample answer:

5 examples: 3-4-5 → 6-8-10 ✓; 5-12-13 → 10-24-26 ✓; 8-15-17 → 16-30-34 ✓; tripled 3-4-5 → 9-12-15 ✓; quadrupled → 12-16-20 ✓.

Rule: If (a, b, c) is a triple, then (ka, kb, kc) is also a triple, for any positive k.

Check: 7-24-25 × 5 = 35-120-125. 1225 + 14400 = 15625 = 125² ✓.

WHY it must be true: (ka)² + (kb)² = k²a² + k²b² = k²(a² + b²) = k²c² = (kc)². Both sides scale by exactly k². The equation is preserved.

Limit: The scaled triples are just bigger copies — they're not new primitive triples like 3-4-5.

Sample 3 — Finding a formula

Question: Find a formula for the long diagonal D of a box with sides a, b, c. Justify it.

Sample answer:

Examples: 4×3×12 → D=13; 2×6×9 → D=11; 6×8×24 → D=26; 1×2×3 → D=√14; 3×4×5 → D=5√2.

Pattern (words): D² always equals the sum of the three sides squared.

Rule: D² = a² + b² + c² (so D = √(a² + b² + c²)).

Check: 5×5×5 cube → D² = 75, so D = 5√3 ✓.

WHY it must be true: Apply Pythagoras twice. First on the base of the box: d² = a² + b². Then on the right triangle made by d and c: D² = d² + c² = (a² + b²) + c². So the rule comes from using 2D Pythagoras twice.

Limit: Only works for cuboids. Other shapes need different formulas.

Common mistakes

  • Only doing 2-3 examples. The marker wants at least 5.
  • Writing the rule in words OR letters, not both.
  • Checking with one of your original examples (that's circular).
  • Stopping at "it worked" without saying WHY. Biggest mark-loser.
  • Forgetting the limit (what the rule does NOT cover).
Criterion D — Real-world problems

Criterion D is about using Pythagoras in real life. The marker wants you to think like a problem-solver, not just a calculator.

Picking the right strategy

When the problem is about…Use…
A ladder, ramp, or sloped linePythagoras 2D
The diagonal of a rectanglePythagoras 2D
Two paths at right angles (like N + E)Pythagoras 2D
The long diagonal of a boxPythagoras 3D: D² = a² + b² + c²
The long diagonal of a cubePythagoras 3D — answer will be s√3
Distance to the horizonPythagoras with Earth's radius + height
Answer isn't a whole numberLeave as a simplified surd

Your check — the 3-part finish

1. Rounding — round to match the inputs. If the question gives 6 m and 8 m (1 s.f.), don't give an answer to 4 s.f.

2. Sanity check — does the answer make sense? Hypotenuse bigger than legs? Right size?

3. Two limits — list 2 things your model leaves out. Use "My model assumes..."

What "limits" to talk about

  • You treated something as a flat line, a point, or a perfect shape (when it isn't).
  • You ignored something real-world (wind, friction, weather, refraction).
  • You assumed something is exact (but real construction has small tolerances).
  • You didn't include effects you'd need to check (safety codes, atmospheric haze).

Sample 1 — Ladder against a wall

Question: A 6 m ladder is placed against a wall with its foot 2 m from the wall. How far up the wall does it reach? Is it safe for cleaning a 5 m gutter?

Sample answer:

Given: Ladder 6 m, foot 2 m out, gutter 5 m. Asked: height reached + safety. Doesn't matter: ladder colour, time of day.

Strategy: Wall vertical, ground horizontal, ladder slanted. Right triangle with ladder as hypotenuse. Pythagoras 2D.

Working: Let h = height. h² + 2² = 6². h² = 32. h = √32 = 4√2 ≈ 5.66 m. ∴ The ladder reaches about 5.7 m up the wall.

Check: Rounding → 5.7 m (2 s.f.). Sanity → ladder (6 m) > height (5.7 m) ✓. Safe for 5 m gutter? Yes, 5.7 > 5.

My model assumes: (1) the wall is exactly vertical and ground exactly flat — in real life either could be slightly off; (2) the ladder is rigid — real ladders bend slightly, reducing the safe reach.

Sample 2 — Box diagonal (3D)

Question: A drone flies diagonally across a 100 m × 60 m field, then climbs 30 m. Find the straight-line distance from start to final position.

Sample answer:

Given: Field 100 × 60 m, climb 30 m. Asked: straight-line distance start to end. Doesn't matter: drone brand, weather.

Strategy: Start and end are at opposite corners of a 100 × 60 × 30 box. The straight-line distance is the long diagonal. 3D Pythagoras.

Working: D² = 100² + 60² + 30² = 14500. D = √14500 = 10√145 ≈ 120.4 m. ∴ Distance is about 120.4 m.

Check: Rounding → 120 m. Sanity → D (120) > longest side (100) ✓.

My model assumes: (1) the drone flies in ONE straight line — in real life it probably flies diagonal then climbs, so actual flight path is longer (≈ 146 m); (2) no wind drift or GPS error; (3) field is perfectly flat.

Sample 3 — Design problem

Question: A school entrance has a 0.4 m step. A wheelchair ramp must have slope ≤ 1:12. 5 m of horizontal space available. Design a ramp and find the slope length.

Sample answer:

Given: Rise 0.4 m, max slope 1:12, space ≤ 5 m. Asked: slope length. Doesn't matter: material, colour.

Strategy: Check fits — 1:12 needs ≥ 12 × 0.4 = 4.8 m. 5 m ≥ 4.8 m ✓. Use run 4.8 m. Slope = hypotenuse. Pythagoras 2D.

Working: L² = 0.4² + 4.8² = 23.2. L = √23.2 ≈ 4.82 m. ∴ Slope is about 4.82 m.

Check: Rounding → 4.8 m (2 s.f.). Sanity → slope (4.82) only slightly more than run (4.8) because rise is small ✓.

My model assumes: (1) the ramp is straight, no landing platforms; (2) the 1:12 rule is the only constraint — real ramps also need handrails and grip surface.

Common mistakes

  • Diving into maths without writing what you're given.
  • Using a strategy without saying WHY.
  • Stopping at the answer. The check is half the marks.
  • Only one or zero limits. The marker wants at least two.
  • Mixing up rounding (decimal places) and sanity check (real-world sense).
